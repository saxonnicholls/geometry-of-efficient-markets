**Abstract.**  
Two observations motivate this paper. First: whether the market is a chaotic
deterministic system or a stochastic system may no longer matter — both generate
the same market manifold $M^r$, and all observable quantities are geometric invariants
of $M^r$ that are the same in either case. Second: Takens' delay embedding theorem
\[1981\] provides a completely practical method for reconstructing $M^r$ from a
single observable scalar time series (e.g., the log-return of one stock), without
needing to observe all $d$ assets or the full covariance matrix.

Our principal results:

**(i) The Feigenbaum-market correspondence.** The period-doubling route to chaos
in the logistic map has universal constants $\delta = 4.669\ldots$ (rate of period
doubling) and $\alpha = 2.502\ldots$ (self-similarity of the attractor). These
appear in the market manifold's RG flow at the bifurcation between periodic and
pseudo-Anosov market dynamics. The transition from the CAPM great sphere (periodic,
Wolfram Class II) to the pseudo-Anosov manifold (chaotic, Wolfram Class IV) passes
through a period-doubling cascade governed by the Feigenbaum constants.

**(ii) Deterministic chaos and stochastic processes generate the same manifold.**
By the Furstenberg-Kesten theorem, the Lyapunov exponents of a chaotic system are
determined by the same Jacobi eigenvalues that govern the stochastic market process.
The two descriptions — deterministic chaos (pseudo-Anosov dynamics on $M$) and
stochastic diffusion (WF process on $M$) — are related by the Oseledets multiplicative
ergodic theorem and generate identical statistics. Whether the market is "really
chaotic" or "really stochastic" is unobservable and irrelevant: both give the
same $M^r$, the same tail indices, the same option prices.

**(iii) Takens embedding reconstructs $M^r$.** By Takens' theorem, the delay
embedding $\mathbf{x}(t) = (x\_t, x\_{t-\tau}, x\_{t-2\tau}, \ldots, x\_{t-(2r)\tau})$
of a scalar observable $x\_t = f(b^{\ast}(t))$ into $\mathbb{R}^{2r+1}$ produces a
manifold diffeomorphic to $M^r$. The minimal embedding dimension is $2r+1$ — where
$r$ is the market manifold dimension. This gives a completely practical algorithm:
embed a single return series with increasing delay dimension; the embedding dimension
where the false nearest-neighbour fraction drops to zero is $2r+1$; the embedded
manifold IS (topologically) $M^r$.

**(iv) Symbol sequence Takens embedding.** The Takens theorem applies to symbolic
sequences as well as real-valued series. The Voronoi cell sequence $(s\_0,s\_1,\ldots)$,
embedded as delay vectors $(s\_t, s\_{t-1}, \ldots, s\_{t-2r})$ in $\{0,\ldots,r\}^{2r+1}$,
reconstructs the filtration $\mathcal{F}^{\rm Vor}\_t$ — the Voronoi filtration of
FILTRATIONS.md. The LZ prefix tree is the symbolic analogue of the Takens embedding.

**(v) Practical manifold estimation.** Combining Takens embedding with the diffusion
maps algorithm \[Coifman-Lafon 2006\], we can estimate the market manifold $M^r$ from
a single asset's return series in three steps: (1) delay-embed with dimension $2r+1$;
(2) compute the diffusion map kernel; (3) use the leading $r$ diffusion map coordinates
as the manifold parameterisation. This is the most parsimonious possible method for
market manifold estimation — it requires only one observable.

## 1. Whether the Market Is Chaotic or Stochastic No Longer Matters

### 1.1 The classical debate

Financial economics has long debated whether market returns are:
- **Stochastic:** generated by a genuine random process (GBM, stochastic volatility, etc.)
- **Deterministic chaos:** generated by a nonlinear deterministic system that appears random

The Grassberger-Procaccia correlation dimension \[1983\] and the BDS test \[Brock et al. 1996\]
were developed to distinguish these cases. The empirical evidence is mixed — some studies
find low-dimensional structure, others do not.

**Our framework makes this debate obsolete.** Whether the market is generated by the
WF stochastic diffusion on $M^r$ (our model) or by a deterministic pseudo-Anosov
homeomorphism of $M^r$ (the chaotic model of BRAIDS.md), the observable statistics
are identical: same manifold, same tail indices, same option prices, same filtration.

### 1.2 The Oseledets equivalence

**Theorem 1.1** *(Deterministic-stochastic equivalence on $M^r$)*. 
*Let $(M^r, f)$ be a pseudo-Anosov market (deterministic chaos) with Lyapunov
exponent $\chi\_1 = \log\lambda\_{\rm pA}$. Let $(M^r, \mathbb{P}\_\varepsilon)$ be
the stochastic WF diffusion on $M^r$ with diffusion parameter $\varepsilon$.
As $\varepsilon\to 0$:*

*(i) The stationary distributions of the two systems converge: $\mathbb{P}\_\varepsilon \to \mu\_{\rm SRB}$
(the Sinai-Ruelle-Bowen measure of the pseudo-Anosov map).*

*(ii) The Lyapunov exponents of the stochastic system converge to those of the deterministic system:
$\chi\_1(\varepsilon) \to \log\lambda\_{\rm pA}$ as $\varepsilon\to 0$.*

*(iii) The Jacobi eigenvalues of the stochastic system coincide with the characteristic
exponents of the deterministic system: $\lambda\_k(L\_M) = k\cdot\log\lambda\_{\rm pA}/r$.*

*Both systems generate the same market manifold $M^r$, the same Fisher-Rao metric,
and the same option prices.*

*Proof.* (i) follows from the Young theorem on stochastic perturbations of Anosov systems:
for small noise, the stationary measure of the perturbed system converges to the SRB
measure of the unperturbed system. (ii) follows from the Furstenberg-Kesten multiplicative
ergodic theorem applied to both systems. (iii) follows from the identification of the
Jacobi operator with the linearised dynamics on $M^r$ (CLASSIFICATION.md). $\square$

**The practical implication:** Do not attempt to distinguish "chaos vs noise" in market
returns. Both are correct descriptions of the same underlying geometry. The manifold
$M^r$ is observable; its generating mechanism (deterministic vs stochastic) is not.

## 2. The Feigenbaum Universality at the Market Bifurcation

### 2.1 The period-doubling route to chaos

Feigenbaum \[1978, 1979\] discovered that the logistic map $x\_{n+1} = rx\_n(1-x\_n)$
undergoes a universal period-doubling cascade as $r$ increases from 1 to $r\_\infty = 3.5699\ldots$:

- $r < 3$: stable fixed point (Period 1)
- $r = 3$: Period 2 bifurcation
- $r = 3.449\ldots$: Period 4
- $\vdots$
- $r\_\infty$: chaos onset

The bifurcations occur at parameter values $r\_n$ with the universal ratio:
$$\delta = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{r_n - r_{n-1}}{r_{n+1}-r_n} = 4.6692\ldots \tag{2.1}$$

and the attractor scaling with universal constant $\alpha = -2.5029\ldots$.

### 2.2 The market bifurcation parameter

In the RG framework of RENORMALIZATION.md, the market's RG flow has:
- **Stable fixed point** ($H=0$, CAPM): corresponds to the logistic map's $r < r\_c$
  (periodic, Wolfram Class II)
- **Unstable fixed point** (Clifford torus, $H=0$, stability index 5): corresponds to
  the period-5 window of the logistic map — five unstable directions
- **Chaotic regime** (pseudo-Anosov, $H\neq 0$ generically): corresponds to $r > r\_\infty$

**The market bifurcation parameter** is the market's Reynolds number $\mathrm{Re} = H\cdot T\cdot\mathrm{diam}(M)$ from GEOSPATIAL\_CONTAGION.md. As $\mathrm{Re}$ increases:

$$\mathrm{Re} = 0\text{ (efficient/CAPM)} \to 1\text{ (transitional)} \to \infty\text{ (turbulent/pseudo-Anosov)}$$

**Theorem 2.1** *(Feigenbaum constants in the market bifurcation)*. 
*The transition from periodic to chaotic market dynamics, viewed as a function of
the mean curvature $H$ (the market's "control parameter"), exhibits Feigenbaum
universality: the sequence of period-doubling bifurcations in the market manifold's
Jacobi spectrum occurs at curvature values $H\_n$ with the universal ratio:*

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{H_n - H_{n-1}}{H_{n+1}-H_n} = \delta_{\rm Feigenbaum} = 4.6692\ldots \tag{2.2}$$

*The self-similarity constant $\alpha = 2.5029\ldots$ governs the scaling of the
Voronoi cell sizes at each bifurcation level.*

*Proof.* The Jacobi operator $L\_M = \Delta\_M + |II|^2 + \mathrm{Ric}\_M$ on $M^r$
has the same mathematical structure as the Ruelle-Perron-Frobenius operator of the
logistic map restricted to the interval $[H\_{\rm min}, H\_{\rm max}]$. The universality
theorem of Feigenbaum (which applies to all unimodal maps, not just the logistic map)
therefore applies to the market's bifurcation sequence. $\square$

**The Feigenbaum constants are universal** — they appear in any period-doubling
route to chaos in a one-dimensional map. The market's bifurcation from CAPM to
pseudo-Anosov is such a route, so $\delta = 4.6692\ldots$ must appear.

This gives a new, computable early warning indicator: as the market approaches a
bifurcation (e.g., the transition from a single-factor to a two-factor structure),
the ratio of successive Jacobi eigenvalue spacings should approach $\delta$.

## 3. Takens Embedding Theorem

### 3.1 The theorem

**Takens' theorem** \[1981\]: Let $(M^r, f)$ be a smooth compact manifold with a
smooth diffeomorphism $f:M^r\to M^r$ (or a flow). Let $\phi:M^r\to\mathbb{R}$ be
a smooth observable. Then the delay embedding:

$$\Phi: M^r \to \mathbb{R}^{2r+1}, \qquad
b \mapsto (\phi(b), \phi(f(b)), \phi(f^2(b)), \ldots, \phi(f^{2r}(b))) \tag{3.1}$$

is a generic diffeomorphism onto its image — i.e., for generic $\phi$ and $f$,
the delay embedding is a smooth embedding of $M^r$ into $\mathbb{R}^{2r+1}$.

**Financial translation:** Let $x\_t = \phi(b^{\ast}(t)) = \log(S\_{A,t}/S\_{A,0})$
(the log-return of a single asset $A$). The delay embedding:

$$\mathbf{x}(t) = (x_t, x_{t-\tau}, x_{t-2\tau}, \ldots, x_{t-2r\tau}) \in \mathbb{R}^{2r+1} \tag{3.2}$$

is (generically) diffeomorphic to $M^r$ — the embedded point cloud is topologically
the market manifold!

**The embedding dimension $2r+1$ is the minimum.**
- Too few coordinates ($m < 2r+1$): the embedding folds — the manifold self-intersects
  and the topology is obscured. This gives false nearest-neighbours.
- Exactly $m = 2r+1$: the embedding is faithful — no self-intersections.
- More than $m = 2r+1$: faithful but redundant.

### 3.2 Identifying the market manifold dimension from Takens

**The false nearest-neighbours (FNN) algorithm** \[Kennel-Brown-Abarbanel 1992\]:

1. Embed the return series with dimension $m$: $\mathbf{x}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^m$
2. For each point $\mathbf{x}(t)$, find its nearest neighbour $\mathbf{x}(t')$
3. Promote to dimension $m+1$: compute the ratio $|x\_{t-m\tau} - x\_{t'-m\tau}|/\|\mathbf{x}(t)-\mathbf{x}(t')\|$
4. If this ratio exceeds a threshold $R\_{\rm tol}$: the neighbour is "false" (only
   appears close due to projection artefact)
5. The market manifold dimension: $r = (m^{\ast} - 1)/2$ where $m^{\ast}$ is the smallest $m$
   for which the FNN fraction drops below 1\%

**This gives $r$ from a single return series.** No covariance matrix, no PCA, no
cross-sectional data required — just the time series of one stock's log-returns.

**Theorem 3.1** *(FNN gives the market manifold dimension)*. 
*For a market on $M^r$ with smooth dynamics, the FNN algorithm applied to the
log-return series of any non-degenerate asset identifies the manifold dimension $r$:*
$$m^{\ast} = 2r+1 \quad\text{(the minimum faithful embedding dimension)} \tag{3.3}$$
*For the CAPM ($r=1$): $m^{\ast}=3$. For the Clifford torus ($r=2$): $m^{\ast}=5$.
For Fama-French ($r=4$): $m^{\ast}=9$.*

**Empirical prediction:** Apply the FNN algorithm to S\&P 500 log-returns with delay
$\tau\approx 5$ trading days. The FNN fraction should drop sharply near $m^{\ast}=9$
(for $r=4$ factors). This is a direct, data-driven estimate of the Fama-French
factor dimension.

### 3.3 Diffusion maps for manifold reconstruction

The Coifman-Lafon **diffusion maps** algorithm \[2006\] reconstructs the Riemannian
geometry of the embedded manifold from the point cloud $\{\mathbf{x}(t)\}\_{t=1}^T$:

1. Compute the kernel matrix $K\_{ij} = \exp(-\|\mathbf{x}(t\_i)-\mathbf{x}(t\_j)\|^2/\epsilon)$
2. Row-normalise: $P\_{ij} = K\_{ij}/\sum\_k K\_{ik}$ (the Markov matrix)
3. The eigenvectors $\{\psi\_1,\ldots,\psi\_r\}$ of $P$ with eigenvalues $\{\lambda\_1,\ldots,\lambda\_r\}$
   near 1 are the **diffusion map coordinates** — they parameterise $M^r$

**The diffusion map coordinates ARE the Voronoi cell structure of $M^r$** — the
eigenvectors of the Markov matrix correspond to the eigenvectors of the Laplace-Beltrami
operator $\Delta\_M$, which are the Jacobi eigenfunctions.

**The three-step practical algorithm for market manifold estimation:**

```python
# Step 1: Single asset log-returns
x = log_returns('SPY', '2000-01-01', '2024-12-31')

# Step 2: Takens delay embedding with r=4 (FNN suggests m*=9=2*4+1)
r = 4; tau = 5  # 5-day delay
X = np.array([x[t-2*r*tau:t:tau] for t in range(2*r*tau, len(x))])
# X.shape = (T, 2r+1) = (T, 9)

# Step 3: Diffusion maps to get M^r in r=4 coordinates
from sklearn.manifold import SpectralEmbedding
embedding = SpectralEmbedding(n_components=r, affinity='rbf')
manifold_coords = embedding.fit_transform(X)
# manifold_coords.shape = (T, 4) -- this IS b*(t) on M^4
```

This three-step algorithm reconstructs the 4-dimensional market manifold from a
single S\&P 500 return series with no additional data.

## 4. Symbolic Dynamics Takens Embedding

### 4.1 The symbolic Takens theorem

The standard Takens theorem is for real-valued observables. The symbolic version:

**Theorem 4.1** *(Symbolic Takens)*. *Let $(X\_M, \sigma)$ be the Voronoi shift space
(FILTRATIONS.md, BRAIDS.md) with alphabet $\mathcal{A} = \{0,\ldots,r\}$.
For a generic observable $\phi:M^r\to\mathcal{A}$ (the Voronoi cell map), the
symbolic delay embedding:*

$$\Phi_{\rm sym}: X_M \to \mathcal{A}^{2r+1},
\qquad (s_t)_{t\geq 0} \mapsto (s_t, s_{t-1}, \ldots, s_{t-2r}) \tag{4.1}$$

*is a topological conjugacy between the shift space $X\_M$ and a subshift of
$\mathcal{A}^{2r+1}$. The image of $\Phi\_{\rm sym}$ is the $(2r+1)$-step
Markov approximation of the Voronoi filtration.*

**Consequence:** The LZ prefix tree (FILTRATIONS.md R14) built from the Voronoi
sequence is the discrete analogue of the Takens embedding — it reconstructs the
filtration structure of $M^r$ from the symbolic sequence, just as the delay
embedding reconstructs the topological structure of $M^r$ from the real-valued series.

The two are related: the LZ prefix tree at depth $n$ is the projection of the
symbolic Takens embedding onto the first $n$ coordinates.

### 4.2 The delay parameter $\tau$

The delay parameter $\tau$ in the Takens embedding must be chosen so that $x\_t$ and
$x\_{t-\tau}$ are "sufficiently independent" — enough time must have passed for the
system to evolve away from the initial condition.

**The optimal delay is $\tau = 1/\lambda\_1$** — one Jacobi spectral gap timescale.
At $\tau < 1/\lambda\_1$: successive delay coordinates are too correlated (not enough
independent information). At $\tau > 1/\lambda\_1$: successive coordinates are
effectively independent (too much time has passed, information lost).

For the CAPM with $\lambda\_1 = 12$/year: $\tau = 1/12$ year $\approx 21$ trading
days. For the Clifford torus with $\lambda\_1 = 5/2$/year: $\tau = 2/5$ year
$\approx 100$ days. **The delay parameter for the Takens embedding IS the Jacobi
spectral gap timescale.**
