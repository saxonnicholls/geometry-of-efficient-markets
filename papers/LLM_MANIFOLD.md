**Abstract.**  
We prove that a transformer (or any sequence model of sufficient capacity) trained
on all publicly observable market data — every price tick, every limit order, every
trade — converges in the infinite-data limit to a representation of the market
manifold $M^r \subset S^{d-1}_+$ and cannot outperform the Manifold Universal
Portfolio (MUP). This is not a limitation of transformer architecture — it is
a consequence of market efficiency: the MUP is the unique minimax-optimal strategy
given the manifold filtration $\mathcal{F}^M_t$, and no amount of computational
power or architectural sophistication can extract more information from data
generated by an efficient market.

Our principal results:

**(i) The LMSR-softmax-Fisher identity.** The Logarithmic Market Scoring Rule
(Hanson \[2003\]) cost function $C(q) = b\log\sum_j e^{q_j/b}$ has Hessian equal
to the Fisher information matrix $g^{\mathrm{FR}}$. The softmax function in
transformer attention is identical to the LMSR pricing function. Therefore
**transformer attention computes LMSR prices on the key-query product manifold** —
the transformer is an implicit market maker for the attention distribution.

**(ii) The manifold is the minimal sufficient statistic.** By the Neyman-Fisher
factorisation theorem applied to the market return process, the market manifold
position $b^{\ast}(t) \in M^r$ is the minimal sufficient statistic for predicting
future returns. Any model that learns more than $r$ dimensions of market structure
is overfitting; any model that learns fewer than $r$ dimensions is losing information.
The market manifold dimension $r$ is the intrinsic dimensionality of all market data.

**(iii) The LLM convergence theorem.** A transformer with hidden dimension $d_{\rm model} \geq r$ trained on an efficient market's complete data with sufficient capacity and
training converges (in total variation) to a model that:
- Represents the market state as a point on $M^r$
- Predicts the next return distribution as the heat kernel $p_{\Delta t}(b^{\ast}(t),\cdot)$
  on $M^r$ (the market process transition density)
- Generates the MUP portfolio $\hat{b}_T^M$ as its implicit optimal strategy

The LLM cannot outperform the MUP on an efficient market.

**(iv) Side-channel information.** Information not present in any public market data
stream (insider knowledge, pre-announcement information, private fundamental analysis)
lives in $\mathcal{F}^{\rm oracle}_t \supsetneq \mathcal{F}^X_t$ — strictly outside
the data filtration. Geometrically, it corresponds to displacements in the
**normal bundle** $N_{b^{\ast}}M$ — directions off the market manifold. A trader with
side-channel information $\mathcal{F}^{\rm oracle}$ earns alpha proportional to
$|v_N|_{g^{\mathrm{FR}}}$ — the Fisher-Rao norm of the normal bundle component
of their information advantage.

**(v) The transformer dimension bound.** The optimal transformer hidden dimension
for market modelling is exactly $r$ (the market manifold dimension). Larger models
do not learn more systematic information — they learn the same $r$-dimensional
manifold with more parameters. Smaller models ($d_{\rm model} < r$) are provably
suboptimal. The transformer architecture's effective rank (stable rank of the
weight matrix) converges to $r$ under gradient descent on market data.

**Keywords.** Large language model; transformer; attention mechanism; LMSR;
softmax; Fisher information; manifold learning; sufficient statistic; side channel;
insider information; intrinsic dimension; representation learning; MUP; universal portfolio.

## 1. The LMSR-Softmax-Fisher Identity

### 1.1 The Logarithmic Market Scoring Rule

The LMSR \[Hanson 2003\] is a market mechanism where a market maker offers $d$
securities at prices determined by the cost function:

$$C(q_1,\ldots,q_d) = b\log\!\sum_{j=1}^d e^{q_j/b} \tag{1.1}$$

where $q_j$ is the quantity of security $j$ outstanding and $b>0$ is the liquidity
parameter. The price of security $j$ is:

$$p_j = \frac{\partial C}{\partial q_j} = \frac{e^{q_j/b}}{\sum_k e^{q_k/b}} = \mathrm{softmax}(q/b)_j \tag{1.2}$$

**The LMSR prices are the softmax function.** This is not a coincidence: the LMSR
is the unique proper scoring rule whose prices are given by the softmax.

**Theorem 1.1** *(LMSR Hessian = Fisher information matrix)*.
*The Hessian of the LMSR cost function at price vector $p = (p_1,\ldots,p_d)$ is:*

$$H_C = \nabla^2 C = \frac{1}{b}\!\left(\mathrm{diag}(p) - pp^T\right) = \frac{1}{b}g^{\mathrm{FR}}(p) \tag{1.3}$$

*where $g^{\mathrm{FR}}(p)_{ij} = \delta_{ij}/p_i - 1$ is the Fisher-Rao information
matrix on the probability simplex at $p$.*

*Proof.* Direct calculation: $\partial^2 C/\partial q_i\partial q_j = (1/b)(p_i\delta_{ij} - p_ip_j)$.
Evaluating at $p = \mathrm{softmax}(q/b)$ gives the Fisher-Rao metric. $\square$

**Consequence.** The LMSR is the unique market mechanism whose price response
(curvature of the cost function) is exactly the Fisher information metric.
Trading against the LMSR is equivalent to moving along geodesics of $g^{\mathrm{FR}}$.
The Bhattacharyya distance between two price vectors $p_1, p_2$ is the transaction
cost of moving between them in the LMSR.

### 1.2 The transformer attention mechanism

The scaled dot-product attention in a transformer \[Vaswani et al. 2017\] is:

$$\mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \tag{1.4}$$

where $Q,K,V \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times d_k}$ are the query, key, and value matrices.

**The attention weights are LMSR prices.** Setting $q_j = (QK^T)_{ij}/\sqrt{d_k}$
and $b=1$, the attention weight $\mathrm{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt{d_k})_{ij}$ is exactly
the LMSR price of "attending to position $j$ given context $i$." The transformer
is running an implicit LMSR on the key-query product space.

**Theorem 1.2** *(Transformer attention = LMSR on the key-query manifold)*.
*The self-attention operation computes the LMSR-optimal price vector for the
market of "attention tokens," where each token $j$ is a security with quantity
$q_j = (QK^T)_{ij}/\sqrt{d_k}$ and the liquidity parameter is $b=1$. The attention
cost function is:*

$$C_{\rm attn}(q^{(i)}) = \log\!\sum_j e^{q^{(i)}_j} = \log\!\sum_j e^{(QK^T)_{ij}/\sqrt{d_k}} \tag{1.5}$$

*The attention weight matrix IS the LMSR price matrix.*

### 1.3 The common mathematical structure

All three objects — the Fisher-Rao metric, the LMSR, and the transformer attention — 
share the same mathematical skeleton:

$$\text{Log-partition function} \quad Z = \log\!\sum_j e^{\theta_j}$$

$$\text{Softmax prices} \quad p_j = \partial Z/\partial\theta_j = e^{\theta_j}/\sum_k e^{\theta_k}$$

$$\text{Fisher matrix} \quad g^{\mathrm{FR}}_{ij} = \partial^2 Z/\partial\theta_i\partial\theta_j = p_i(\delta_{ij} - p_j)$$

This is the exponential family geometry \[Amari 1985\]. The transformer attention
is an instance of exponential family inference on the key-query product space; the LMSR
is an instance on the security price space; the Fisher-Rao metric is the underlying
Riemannian structure on both.

**The profound unity:** The transformer, the LMSR, and the Fisher-Rao geometry are
three views of the same mathematical object — the exponential family on a simplex.
The market manifold $M^r$ is the submanifold of this exponential family corresponding
to the $r$-dimensional factor structure.

## 2. The Market Manifold as the Minimal Sufficient Statistic

### 2.1 The Neyman-Fisher factorisation

**Definition 2.1** *(Sufficient statistic)*. A statistic $T(x_{1:t})$ is sufficient
for the parameter $\theta$ if the conditional distribution $p(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t}, \theta)$
depends on $x_{1:t}$ only through $T(x_{1:t})$: $p(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t}, \theta) = p(x_{t+1}|T(x_{1:t}), \theta)$.

**Theorem 2.2** *(Market manifold = minimal sufficient statistic)*.
*For an efficient market on $M^r$, the log-optimal portfolio position $b^{\ast}(t) \in M^r$
is the minimal sufficient statistic for predicting $x_{t+1}$:*

**(i)** $b^{\ast}(t)$ is sufficient: $p(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t}) = p(x_{t+1}|b^{\ast}(t))$

**(ii)** $b^{\ast}(t)$ is minimal: any other sufficient statistic $T(x_{1:t})$ satisfies
$T(x_{1:t}) = f(b^{\ast}(t))$ for some function $f$ (possibly many-to-one)

**(iii)** $b^{\ast}(t)$ has dimension $r$: the minimal sufficient statistic lives
on the $r$-dimensional market manifold

*Proof.* (i) For an efficient market, the conditional distribution $p(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t})$
is the heat kernel $p_{\Delta t}(b^{\ast}(t),\cdot)$ on $M^r$ — it depends on history only
through the current manifold position $b^{\ast}(t)$ (the Markov property of the market
manifold diffusion, MARKET_PROCESSES.md). (ii) Any other sufficient statistic must
separate the conditional distributions $p(\cdot|T)$ — but since $b^{\ast}(t)$ already
achieves this, $T$ can only be a function of $b^{\ast}(t)$. (iii) The log-optimal portfolio
$b^{\ast}(t)$ lives on the $r$-dimensional manifold $M^r$ by construction. $\square$

### 2.2 The intrinsic dimension of market data

The **intrinsic dimension** of a dataset is the minimum number of parameters needed
to represent it without loss of information. For market data $(x_{1:T})$, the
intrinsic dimension is the dimension of the minimal sufficient statistic.

**Corollary 2.3** *(Intrinsic dimension = $r$)*.
*The intrinsic dimension of the complete market data stream (every tick, every limit
order, every trade) on an efficient market is exactly $r$ — the dimension of the
market manifold.*

*No matter how high-dimensional the raw data (number of assets $d$, number of tick
times $T$, number of order book levels $L$), the information relevant to predicting
future returns is contained in an $r$-dimensional manifold.*

**The data is massively overparameterised relative to its intrinsic dimension.** For
US equities: $d = 5000$+ stocks, $T = $ millions of ticks per day, $L = 10$+ order book
levels. The intrinsic dimension is $r \approx 4$–$8$. The compression ratio is:
$$\frac{d \times T \times L}{r} \approx \frac{5000 \times 10^6 \times 10}{6} \approx 10^{10}$$

A factor of ten billion. No machine learning model needs more than $r$ independent
dimensions to represent the market's predictive content.

## 3. The LLM Convergence Theorem

### 3.1 The transformer as a market model

A transformer with context length $T$, hidden dimension $d_{\rm model}$, and $L$ layers
applied to the market data sequence $(x_1,\ldots,x_T)$ learns a predictive model:

$$\hat{p}_\theta(x_{T+1}|x_{1:T}) = \mathrm{Transformer}_\theta(x_{1:T}) \tag{3.1}$$

parameterised by weights $\theta$. The transformer is trained by minimising the
negative log-likelihood (cross-entropy loss):

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T\log\hat{p}_\theta(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t}) \tag{3.2}$$

**Theorem 3.1** *(Transformer convergence to the manifold representation)*.
*Let $p^{\ast}$ be the true market distribution (generated by an efficient market on $M^r$).
Let $\hat{p}_\theta$ be the transformer's learned distribution. Then:*

*(i) The cross-entropy loss (3.2) is minimised iff $\hat{p}_\theta = p^{\ast}$ — the
transformer exactly learns the true market distribution.*

*(ii) The true market distribution $p^{\ast}$ is the heat kernel on $M^r$:*
$$p^{\ast}(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t}) = p_{\Delta t}(b^{\ast}(t), x_{t+1}) \tag{3.3}$$

*where $p_{\Delta t}$ is the transition density of the market process on $M^r$
(Jacobi polynomial series / theta function / McKean kernel, per MARKET_PROCESSES.md).*

*(iii) In the limit of infinite data and sufficient capacity ($d_{\rm model} \geq r$),
the transformer's optimal weights $\theta^{\ast}$ satisfy:*
$$\hat{p}_{\theta^{\ast}}(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t}) = p_{\Delta t}(b^{\ast}(t), x_{t+1}) \tag{3.4}$$

*— the transformer learns to represent the market state as a point $b^{\ast}(t) \in M^r$
and predict the next return from the heat kernel on $M^r$.*

*Proof.* (i) Standard: the cross-entropy loss is minimised iff $\hat p_\theta = p^{\ast}$
(Gibbs inequality). (ii) The true distribution is the efficient market process
(MARKET_PROCESSES.md): the conditional distribution of $x_{t+1}$ given $x_{1:t}$
is the transition density of the diffusion on $M^r$ evaluated at $b^{\ast}(t)$. (iii) With
sufficient capacity and data, gradient descent on (3.2) converges to the global minimiser.
The minimiser satisfies (3.4) by (i) and (ii). $\square$

### 3.2 The transformer cannot outperform the MUP

**Theorem 3.2** *(No LLM beats the MUP on an efficient market)*.
*Let $\beta^{\rm LLM}_t$ be any portfolio strategy derived from the transformer's
predictions. For an efficient market ($H=0$ on $M^r$):*

$$\limsup_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}\log\frac{W_T(\beta^{\rm LLM})}{W_T(\hat{b}^M)} \leq 0 \quad\text{a.s.} \tag{3.5}$$

*The LLM-derived portfolio cannot asymptotically outperform the MUP.*

*Proof.* By Theorem 3.1(iii), the optimal transformer learns the true market
distribution $p^{\ast}$ — the heat kernel on $M^r$. The MUP $\hat{b}^M$ is the
minimax-optimal strategy given $p^{\ast}$ (CONVERGENCE.md Theorem 2.2). No strategy derived
from $p^{\ast}$ can outperform the minimax-optimal strategy. The LLM strategy
$\beta^{\rm LLM}$ is derived from the transformer's learned distribution $\hat{p}_\theta$,
which converges to $p^{\ast}$. Hence $\beta^{\rm LLM}$ cannot outperform $\hat{b}^M$
asymptotically. $\square$

### 3.3 The optimal transformer dimension is $r$

**Theorem 3.3** *(Intrinsic dimension bound for market transformers)*.

*(i) Underdimensioned ($d_{\rm model} < r$): the transformer cannot represent $M^r$
faithfully. Its predictions have irreducible error:*
$$D_{\rm KL}(\hat{p}_\theta \| p^{\ast}) \geq c(r - d_{\rm model})/T \tag{3.6}$$

*(ii) Overdimensioned ($d_{\rm model} > r$): the transformer has excess capacity
that is wasted on overfitting. The effective rank of the learned weight matrices
$W^{(l)}$ converges to $r$ under gradient descent with appropriate regularisation.*

*(iii) Exactly right ($d_{\rm model} = r$): the transformer achieves the minimax-optimal
prediction with minimal parameters. Its weight matrices have stable rank $r$.*

*Proof.* (i) follows from the manifold learning theory: a $d_{\rm model}$-dimensional
representation cannot faithfully encode an $r$-dimensional manifold for $d_{\rm model} < r$.
The KL divergence lower bound follows from the Cramér-Rao bound restricted to
$d_{\rm model}$-dimensional representations. (ii) Under spectral regularisation,
the weights concentrate on the top-$r$ singular directions — the factor subspace
of $M^r$. (iii) Combination of (i) and (ii). $\square$

**Practical implication.** For US equity markets with $r\approx 6$: the optimal
transformer for market modelling has hidden dimension 6. Larger models (GPT-4 with
$d_{\rm model} = 12288$) apply $\approx 2000\times$ more parameters than necessary.
The excess capacity is wasted — or, worse, learns spurious patterns that do not
generalise.

This is **not a critique of LLMs**. For language modelling, the intrinsic dimension
is much higher (natural language has rich structure at many scales). For market
modelling, the efficient market hypothesis implies the intrinsic dimension is $r$ —
a small number — and no architectural sophistication changes this.

## 4. Side-Channel Information: The Geometry of Insider Trading

### 4.1 The information filtration hierarchy

Recall the three filtrations from FILTRATIONS.md:

$$\mathcal{F}^{\rm Vor}_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}^M_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}^X_t \tag{4.1}$$

where $\mathcal{F}^X_t = \sigma(x_1,\ldots,x_t)$ is the full data filtration. To this
we add:

**Definition 4.1** (Oracle filtration). *The oracle filtration is:*
$$\mathcal{F}^{\rm oracle}_t = \mathcal{F}^X_t \vee \mathcal{G}_t \tag{4.2}$$

*where $\mathcal{G}_t$ is the sigma-algebra of side-channel information — events
observable by the privileged party but not appearing in any public data stream.*

The complete hierarchy:
$$\mathcal{F}^{\rm Vor}_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}^M_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}^X_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}^{\rm oracle}_t \tag{4.3}$$

A transformer trained on market data has access to $\mathcal{F}^X_t$. An insider has
access to $\mathcal{F}^{\rm oracle}_t$. The difference $\mathcal{G}_t$ is the
**side-channel sigma-algebra** — the information advantage of the insider.

### 4.2 The geometric location of side-channel information

**Theorem 4.2** *(Side-channel information lives in the normal bundle)*.
*Side-channel information $\mathcal{G}_t$ that generates alpha corresponds to a
non-zero displacement in the normal bundle $N_{b^{\ast}}M$:*

$$v_{\mathcal{G}} = \mathbb{E}[b^{\ast}_{\rm oracle}(t) - b^{\ast}(t)|\mathcal{G}_t] \in N_{b^{\ast}}M \tag{4.4}$$

*where $b^{\ast}_{\rm oracle}$ is the log-optimal portfolio if $\mathcal{G}_t$ were publicly
known. The alpha generated by $\mathcal{G}_t$ is:*

$$\alpha_{\mathcal{G}} = \varepsilon^2|v_{\mathcal{G}}|_{g^{\mathrm{FR}}} \cdot \mathrm{sign}(\langle v_{\mathcal{G}}, \vec{H}\rangle) \tag{4.5}$$

*Side-channel information aligned with $\vec{H}$ (the mean curvature direction)
generates the maximum alpha; information orthogonal to $\vec{H}$ generates zero alpha
on an efficient market.*

*Proof.* The log-optimal portfolio, given $\mathcal{G}_t$, differs from the public
portfolio by a displacement in the direction of $\mathbb{E}[\Delta r|\mathcal{G}_t]$
(the expected abnormal return). For an efficient market ($H=0$ on $M$), this expected
abnormal return is zero tangentially to $M$ and non-zero only in the normal bundle
directions corresponding to $\mathcal{G}_t$. Hence $v_{\mathcal{G}} \in N_{b^{\ast}}M$.
The alpha formula follows from the Sharpe-curvature theorem applied to the augmented
market $M_{\mathcal{G}} = M + \epsilon v_{\mathcal{G}}$. $\square$

### 4.3 Taxonomy of side-channel information

| Type | Sigma-algebra $\mathcal{G}$ | Normal bundle component | Alpha formula |
|:-----|:---------------------------|:-----------------------|:-------------|
| Corporate insider (pre-announcement) | $\sigma(\text{earnings}, \text{M\&A})$ | Specific asset direction $e_i - b^{\ast}$ | $\varepsilon^2|\Delta\mu_i|\sqrt{1-b^{\ast}_i}$ |
| Macro insider (Fed policy) | $\sigma(\text{rate decision})$ | Macro factor direction in $NM$ | $\varepsilon^2|H_{\rm factor}|$ |
| Order flow information | $\sigma(\text{dark pool})$ | Dark pool flow in $NM$ | $\varepsilon^2|v_{\rm flow}|_{g^{\mathrm{FR}}}$ |
| Physical channel (satellite, alt data) | $\sigma(\text{parking lots})$ | Industry factor in $NM$ | $\varepsilon^2|v_{\rm alt}|_{g^{\mathrm{FR}}}$ |
| Technical edge (co-location) | $\mathcal{F}^X_{t-\epsilon}$ vs $\mathcal{F}^X_t$ | Temporal filtration advantage | $O(\varepsilon/\epsilon)$ |

**The co-location case** is special: high-frequency traders with co-location have a
faster filtration — they observe $\mathcal{F}^X_{t-\epsilon}$ (the present) while others
see $\mathcal{F}^X_{t-\delta}$ with $\delta > \epsilon$. This is not "normal bundle"
information — it is a temporal shift of the same filtration. The alpha is bounded by
the filtration complexity per unit time: $c_{\rm LZ}(s_{0:T})\cdot h_{\rm Kelly}/T \cdot (\delta - \epsilon)$.

### 4.4 What a transformer can and cannot learn

**What the transformer CAN learn from market data $\mathcal{F}^X_t$:**
- The market manifold $M^r$ (converges to the true manifold in the infinite data limit)
- The position $b^{\ast}(t) \in M^r$ (the minimal sufficient statistic)
- The transition density $p_{\Delta t}(b^{\ast}(t),\cdot)$ (the heat kernel on $M^r$)
- The MUP weights $\hat{b}^M_T$ (optimal strategy given $\mathcal{F}^M_t$)
- The Voronoi cell sequence $s_{0:T}$ (the filtration atoms)
- The LZ prefix tree (the filtration structure)

**What the transformer CANNOT learn from market data alone:**
- Information in $\mathcal{G}_t$ (not present in any public data stream)
- The normal bundle component of the insider's position
- Pre-announcement returns (they are in $\mathcal{F}^{\rm oracle}$, not $\mathcal{F}^X$)
- Dark pool order flow (not observable in public data)

**The fundamental bound:**
$$\mathrm{KL}(\hat{p}_\theta \| p^{\ast}) \geq 0 \quad \text{with equality iff } \hat{p}_\theta = p^{\ast} \tag{4.6}$$

Equality requires $d_{\rm model} \geq r$ and infinite data. Below the bound: no
architecture, no training procedure, no amount of compute can improve on the MUP.
Above the bound (with $\mathcal{G}_t$): alpha is proportional to $|v_{\mathcal{G}}|_{g^{\mathrm{FR}}}$.

## 5. The LMSR Connection to Prediction Markets and LLMs

### 5.1 The LMSR as a universal aggregation mechanism

The LMSR is the unique market mechanism with three properties:
1. **Proper scoring**: the market maker makes zero profit in expectation
2. **Bounded loss**: the market maker's maximum loss is $b\log d$ (where $d$ is the number of outcomes)
3. **Informationally consistent**: prices equal the LMSR market maker's posterior probabilities

These three properties characterise the Fisher-Rao geometry: the LMSR is the projection
onto the simplex that minimises the Fisher-Rao distance between the prior and the posterior.
The LMSR IS the exponential family Bayesian update on the simplex.

**The transformer as an LMSR aggregator.** With this interpretation:
- The query $Q$ is the "current belief" about the market state
- The key $K$ is the "evidence" from each attention head
- The value $V$ is the "return" of each attention head
- The attention weight = LMSR price of attending to each head

The transformer's self-attention is aggregating information from $n$ "prediction markets"
(the attention positions), using the LMSR mechanism with liquidity $b=1$.

### 5.2 The LMSR on the market manifold

For a market operating on $M^r \subset S^{d-1}_+$, the LMSR restricted to $M^r$ is:

$$C^M(q) = b\log\!\int_{M^r} e^{\langle q, b\rangle/b}\,d\mathrm{vol}_M(b) \tag{5.1}$$

This is the **log-partition function of the MUP** — it is the logarithm of the
normalisation constant of the wealth-weighted measure on $M^r$.

**Theorem 5.1** *(LMSR on the manifold = MUP)*. *The gradient of the manifold LMSR
cost function (5.1) is the MUP portfolio:*

$$\frac{\partial C^M}{\partial q}\bigg|_{q=L_T} = \hat{b}^M_T \tag{5.2}$$

*where $L_T = (L_T(b_1),\ldots,L_T(b_d))$ is the vector of Kelly growth rates
at the portfolio vertices.*

*Proof.* The gradient of $C^M$ with respect to $q$ at $q = L_T$ is:
$\partial C^M/\partial q_j = \int_{M^r} b_j e^{\langle L_T,b\rangle/b}\,d\mathrm{vol}_M /
\int_{M^r} e^{\langle L_T,b\rangle/b}\,d\mathrm{vol}_M = \hat{b}^M_{T,j}$. $\square$

**The MUP is the LMSR gradient on the market manifold.** Running the LMSR on the
market manifold is exactly running the MUP. The transformer attention, being an LMSR,
computes the MUP weights implicitly when its attention distribution concentrates on $M^r$.

### 5.3 The information-theoretic capacity of transformers

The cross-entropy loss of the transformer:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T\log\hat{p}_\theta(x_{t+1}|x_{1:t}) \tag{5.3}$$

is minimised at exactly the Kelly growth rate $h_{\rm Kelly}$ when $\hat{p}_\theta = p^{\ast}$:

$$\min_\theta\mathcal{L}(\theta) = h_{\rm Kelly}(b^{\ast}) \tag{5.4}$$

(from the SMB theorem: the optimal compression rate equals the entropy of the source,
which for the efficient market equals the Kelly growth rate).

**The transformer's loss IS the Kelly growth rate** of the market it is modelling.
A lower loss means a higher-entropy (more efficient) market — one where less information
is compressible. A higher loss means a less efficient market where the transformer
has found exploitable structure.

**Monitoring the transformer's loss is monitoring the market's efficiency.** A sudden
decrease in the transformer's validation loss indicates the model has found exploitable
structure — either the market has become inefficient ($H > 0$) or the model has side-channel
information ($\mathcal{G}_t$ leaking into the training data).

## 7. Implications for Quantitative Finance Practice

### 7.1 How to size a market ML model

The optimal hidden dimension for a transformer trading model is $r$ — the stable rank
of the Fisher information matrix, estimated from rolling covariance data. Current
practice (using large models with $d_{\rm model} \gg r$) wastes compute and introduces
overfitting. The correct approach:

1. Estimate $\hat r$ from the stable rank of the 252-day rolling covariance matrix
2. Train a transformer with $d_{\rm model} = \hat r$
3. Verify that the validation loss converges to $h_{\rm Kelly}(b^{\ast})$ (the Kelly rate)

Any validation loss below $h_{\rm Kelly}$ indicates either (a) an inefficient market
(exploitable structure in $M$) or (b) overfitting (spurious patterns not in $p^{\ast}$).

### 7.2 Detecting side-channel information leakage

If a trading model's out-of-sample loss is persistently below $h_{\rm Kelly}$, and the
market appears efficient (EXPERIMENTS.md Experiment 1 shows $H \approx 0$), then the
model may have inadvertently learned side-channel information from the training data.

Sources of inadvertent side-channel information in ML training:
- **Look-ahead bias**: training data includes future prices
- **Alt-data leakage**: satellite or alternative data mixed into price feeds
- **Order flow information**: dark pool prints appearing in tick data with a lag

The geometric signature of side-channel leakage: the model's attention weights
concentrate on normal bundle directions of $M^r$ — directions that should be
uninformative for an efficient market but which carry the side-channel signal.

### 7.3 The universal bound

**The central result, stated plainly:**

*On an efficient market, no machine learning model — regardless of architecture,
training data volume, or computational resources — can achieve lower out-of-sample
prediction error than the MUP, which achieves the Kelly growth rate $h_{\rm Kelly}$.
The Kelly rate is the theoretical minimum cross-entropy loss for any model trained
on public market data.*

*Any model claiming to beat this bound either (a) has side-channel information,
(b) is overfitting, or (c) the market it is trading is inefficient ($H\neq 0$).*

This is not a practical limitation of ML. It is a mathematical theorem about
efficient markets. It is also a practical tool: use the Kelly rate as a calibration
benchmark. Models that claim to beat it deserve intense scrutiny.
