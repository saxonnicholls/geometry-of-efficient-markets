**Abstract.**
Three results that unify the trader, the market, and the information channel
into a single geometric framework.

**(i) The observer's price depends on their position on the manifold.**
A trader at portfolio $b^{(\rm obs)}$ experiences asset $i$'s price sensitivity
as $F_{ii}(b^{(\rm obs)}) = 1/b_i^{(\rm obs)}$ — the Fisher-Rao metric at
THEIR position. Two traders holding different portfolios literally experience
different prices for the same asset. The "market price" is not a property of
the asset — it is a property of the observer-asset pair.

**(ii) Every trade creates a shared filtration that is coarser than
either trader's private information.** When traders A and B transact, they
reveal the trade filtration $\mathcal{F}^{\rm trade}_{t} = \sigma(p_s, q_s : s \leq t)$
— a proper subfiltration of both $\mathcal{F}^{A}_t$ and $\mathcal{F}^{B}_t$.
The shared filtration $\mathcal{F}^{A \cap B}_{t}$ sits between the trade
filtration and the private filtrations. The information that LEAKS through
the shared filtration is measured by Kyle's lambda, which IS the Fisher
information of the trade about the true value
(MARKET_MICROSTRUCTURE.md Theorem M3).

**(iii) The convex ambient space provides information shortcuts that
the manifold does not.** On the manifold $M^r$, information propagates at
speed $c_M = \lambda_1 / \text{diam}_{G}$ (the spectral gap divided by the
graph diameter). Through the ambient simplex $\Delta_{d-1}$, information
can take a shortcut: the ambient geodesic between two points is SHORTER
than the manifold geodesic whenever the manifold is curved. The shortcut
capacity is proportional to the Willmore energy $\mathcal{W}$. A market
maker who holds an off-manifold portfolio IS a relay in the ambient channel.
The optimal market has $\mathcal{W} > 0$ — not perfectly efficient but
efficient enough that the ambient shortcuts balance the manifold's
intrinsic information flow.

**Keywords.** Observer; filtration; shared filtration; ambient channel;
relay channel; shortcut; Willmore energy; market maker; Kyle's lambda;
Fisher information; connected sum; intermarket; convex hull; CFL condition.

## 1. The Observer on the Manifold

### 1.1 The observer's metric

A trader's state is their portfolio $b^{(\rm obs)} \in \Delta_{d-1}$.
Their experience of the market is filtered through the Fisher-Rao metric
AT THEIR POSITION:

$$g^{\rm FR}_{ij}(b^{(\rm obs)}) = \frac{\delta_{ij}}{b_i^{(\rm obs)}} \tag{1.1}$$

This means:
- An asset with large weight ($b_i$ large): $g_{ii} = 1/b_i$ is SMALL.
  The observer is INSENSITIVE to small changes. They are "far from the
  boundary" in this direction.
- An asset with small weight ($b_i$ small): $g_{ii} = 1/b_i$ is LARGE.
  The observer is HYPERSENSITIVE. They are "near the Feller boundary."

**Two observers at different points experience different curvatures,
different spectral gaps, and different Sharpe ratios** for the same
underlying market. The Sharpe-curvature identity $\mathrm{Sharpe}^{\ast} = \|H\|$
holds at each point, but $\|H\|$ VARIES across the manifold.

### 1.2 Why this matters

**Large holders move markets slowly.** A pension fund with $b_{\rm AAPL} = 0.05$
has $g_{\rm AAPL,AAPL} = 20$. A retail investor with $b_{\rm AAPL} = 0.001$
has $g_{\rm AAPL,AAPL} = 1000$ — they are 50× more sensitive. The retail
investor reacts to a 1% move in Apple the way the pension fund reacts to a
50% move.

**This is not a behavioural bias — it is geometry.** The retail investor is
CORRECTLY more sensitive because a 1% move in Apple is a LARGER fraction of
their portfolio. The Fisher-Rao metric captures this correctly.

## 2. The Shared Filtration

### 2.1 Three filtrations at every trade

When trader A and trader B transact at time $t$:

**Private filtrations:**
$$\mathcal{F}^{A}_t = \sigma(\text{everything A knows at time } t) \tag{2.1a}$$
$$\mathcal{F}^{B}_t = \sigma(\text{everything B knows at time } t) \tag{2.1b}$$

**The shared filtration:**
$$\mathcal{F}^{A \cap B}_{t} = \mathcal{F}^{A}_t \cap \mathcal{F}^{B}_t \tag{2.2}$$

The set of events that BOTH traders can determine. This includes:
- Public information (prices, news, filings)
- Common knowledge (both know that both know the price)
- Structural knowledge (the market manifold dimension $r$, the factor loadings)

**The trade filtration:**
$$\mathcal{F}^{\rm trade}_{t} = \sigma(p_s, q_s, \text{timestamps} : s \leq t) \tag{2.3}$$

Generated by the tape — the sequence of prices, sizes, and times.

### 2.2 The filtration hierarchy

**Theorem 2.1** *(Filtration ordering at a trade)*.

$$\mathcal{F}^{\rm trade}_{t} \subseteq \mathcal{F}^{A \cap B}_{t}
 \subseteq \mathcal{F}^{A}_t \cap \mathcal{F}^{B}_t
 \subseteq \mathcal{F}^{A}_t, \mathcal{F}^{B}_t \tag{2.4}$$

*Each inclusion is strict in general.*

*Proof.*
(i) $\mathcal{F}^{\rm trade} \subseteq \mathcal{F}^{A \cap B}$: the trade
outcomes $(p_s, q_s)$ are measurable with respect to both $\mathcal{F}^{A}$
and $\mathcal{F}^{B}$ (both traders observe the trade), hence measurable with
respect to their intersection.

(ii) The inclusion is strict: both traders know the current VIX level (which
is in $\mathcal{F}^{A \cap B}$) but it is not revealed by any individual
trade (not in $\mathcal{F}^{\rm trade}$).

(iii) $\mathcal{F}^{A \cap B} \subseteq \mathcal{F}^{A} \cap \mathcal{F}^{B}$
is trivial.

(iv) Strictness: A may know something B doesn't (A has a proprietary model),
so $\mathcal{F}^{A} \setminus \mathcal{F}^{B} \neq \varnothing$. $\square$

### 2.3 The information that leaks

The trade does not reveal either trader's private signal directly. But it
reveals SOMETHING — the size and direction of the trade carry information
about the private signals.

**Definition 2.2** (Information leakage). *The information leakage of the
trade to an observer with filtration $\mathcal{G}_{t}$ is:*

$$\ell_t = I(\mathcal{F}^{A}_t \cup \mathcal{F}^{B}_t ; \mathcal{F}^{\rm trade}_{t} | \mathcal{G}_{t}) \tag{2.5}$$

*— the mutual information between the traders' combined private information
and the trade outcome, given the observer's background knowledge.*

For a market maker observing the tape: $\mathcal{G}_{t} = \mathcal{F}^{\rm trade}_{t-1}$
(they know the history of trades). The leakage per trade is Kyle's lambda:

$$\ell_t = \frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 \cdot \text{Var}(q_t) \tag{2.6}$$

where $\lambda$ is Kyle's price impact coefficient. From
MARKET_MICROSTRUCTURE.md Theorem M3: $\lambda = (1/2) \cdot I_F(v; x)$ —
half the Fisher information of the order flow about the true value.

### 2.4 The shared filtration on the manifold

Geometrically, the shared filtration corresponds to a COMMON VORONOI
PARTITION. Each trader has their own Voronoi partition of the manifold
(adapted to their factor model, their information, their position):

$$\mathcal{V}^{A} = \{V^A_1, \ldots, V^A_{N_A}\}, \qquad
\mathcal{V}^{B} = \{V^B_1, \ldots, V^B_{N_B}\}$$

The shared partition is the COARSEST COMMON REFINEMENT:

$$\mathcal{V}^{A \cap B} = \{V^A_i \cap V^B_j : V^A_i \cap V^B_j \neq \varnothing\}$$

This has at most $N_A \cdot N_B$ cells — but typically many fewer, because
the traders' partitions are correlated (both are adapted to the same
underlying manifold).

**The number of cells in the shared partition measures the RESOLUTION of the
shared information.** More cells = finer shared knowledge. Fewer cells =
coarser shared knowledge.

## 3. The Trader as a Manifold

### 3.1 Every trader IS an intermarket

Each trader $k$ has:
- A manifold $M^{r_k}_{k}$ (the set of portfolios they'd hold under different states)
- A spectral gap $\lambda_1^{(k)}$ (how fast they process information)
- A Cheeger constant $h_M^{(k)}$ (how well-connected their information sources are)
- A Willmore energy $\mathcal{W}_{k}$ (how far they are from their own efficient frontier)

A trade between traders A and B creates a NECK between $M_A$ and $M_B$ — exactly
the connected sum from INTERMARKET_GEOMETRY.md:

$$M_{\rm trade} = M_A \#_{(p_t, q_t)} M_B \tag{3.1}$$

The neck has:
- **Width** $\propto q_t$ (trade size — larger trades create wider necks)
- **Position** = the traded asset (which dimension of the simplex the neck connects)
- **Curvature** = the spread (bid-ask = Fisher-Rao distance at the neck)

### 3.2 The market is a connected sum of all traders

The market at time $t$ is:

$$M_{\rm market}(t) = \#_{k=1}^{K} M_k \tag{3.2}$$

with necks between every pair of traders who have EVER traded. The
topology evolves:
- New traders enter → new manifold components added
- Traders transact → necks created or widened
- Traders exit → components removed (but necks to other traders persist
  in the information structure)

**The market's spectral gap emerges from the topology of the connected sum:**

$$\lambda_1(\text{market}) = \min\!\left(\min_k \lambda_1^{(k)}, \min_{\text{necks}} \frac{\pi^2}{L_{\rm neck}^{2}}\right) \tag{3.3}$$

The market is only as fast as its slowest component or thinnest neck.

## 4. Ambient Shortcuts

### 4.1 The manifold geodesic vs the ambient chord

On $M^r$: the geodesic from $p$ to $q$ has length $d_M(p, q)$.
In $S^{d-1}_{+}$: the great circle arc has length $d_{\rm amb}(p, q) \leq d_M(p, q)$.

The **shortcut factor:**

$$\mathcal{R}(p, q) = \frac{d_M(p, q)}{d_{\rm amb}(p, q)} \geq 1 \tag{4.1}$$

Equality iff the geodesic on $M^r$ IS a geodesic of the ambient sphere (totally
geodesic — the CAPM case).

For a curved manifold: $\mathcal{R} > 1$, and the ambient provides a genuine shortcut.

### 4.2 The ambient channel

A trader who temporarily holds a portfolio OUTSIDE $M^r$ — in the convex hull
$\text{conv}(M^r)$ — is using the ambient channel. They leave the efficient
manifold, take a shortcut through the ambient simplex, and return to the
manifold at a different point.

**Market makers do this continuously.** A market maker holds inventory
(an off-manifold portfolio) to facilitate trades. Their inventory IS
the relay in the ambient channel. The bid-ask spread is the COST of
relaying through the ambient.

**Theorem 4.1** *(Ambient channel capacity)*.
*The information capacity of the ambient channel between points $p, q \in M^r$
exceeds the manifold channel capacity by the shortcut factor:*

$$C_{\rm amb}(p, q) = C_{\rm manifold}(p, q) \cdot \mathcal{R}(p, q) \tag{4.2}$$

*The total ambient capacity available to the market is:*

$$C_{\rm amb}^{\rm total} = C_{\rm manifold}^{\rm total} + C_{\rm shortcut}$$

*where the shortcut capacity is proportional to the Willmore energy:*

$$C_{\rm shortcut} \propto \mathcal{W}(M^r) = \int_{M^r}\|H\|^2\,d\mathrm{vol} \tag{4.3}$$

*Proof sketch.* The shortcut capacity depends on how much "room" the ambient
space provides beyond the manifold. This room is measured by the extrinsic
curvature — how much the manifold curves away from ambient geodesics. The
integrated squared curvature is the Willmore energy. $\square$

### 4.3 Why perfect efficiency is suboptimal

If $\mathcal{W} = 0$ (the manifold is minimal, the market is perfectly efficient):
- $C_{\rm shortcut} = 0$ — no ambient channels available
- All information must flow along the manifold geodesics at speed $\lambda_1$
- New information can only be incorporated at rate $C_{\rm manifold}$

If $\mathcal{W} > 0$ (the market has some inefficiency):
- $C_{\rm shortcut} > 0$ — ambient channels are available
- Market makers provide relay capacity through the ambient
- Total information throughput = $C_{\rm manifold} + C_{\rm shortcut}$

**Theorem 4.2** *(Optimal inefficiency)*.
*The total information capacity of a market is maximised at a positive
Willmore energy $\mathcal{W}^{\ast} > 0$. The optimal inefficiency balances
the ambient shortcut capacity against the cost of being off-manifold:*

$$\mathcal{W}^{\ast} = \arg\max_{\mathcal{W}} \left[C_{\rm geo}(\lambda_1) + C_{\rm shortcut}(\mathcal{W}) - \text{Cost}(\mathcal{W})\right] \tag{4.4}$$

*At the optimum: $\partial C_{\rm shortcut}/\partial\mathcal{W} = \partial\text{Cost}/\partial\mathcal{W}$
— the marginal shortcut capacity equals the marginal cost of inefficiency.*

**The market does NOT converge to perfect efficiency because perfect efficiency
closes the ambient channels.** The MCF drives $\mathcal{W}$ downward, but
as $\mathcal{W} \to 0$, the ambient channels close, information slows, new
inefficiencies form faster than they're removed, and $\mathcal{W}$ stabilises
at a positive equilibrium $\mathcal{W}^{\ast}$.

### 4.4 The Convex Hull Portfolio

The MUP integrates over $M^r$. Cover's portfolio integrates over $\Delta_{d-1}$.
Between them:

**Definition 4.3** (Convex Hull Portfolio, CHP). *The CHP integrates over the
convex hull of the market manifold:*

$$b^{\rm CHP}_{T} = \frac{\int_{\rm conv(M^r)} b\, W_T(b)\, d\mathrm{vol}(b)}{\int_{\rm conv(M^r)} W_T(b)\, d\mathrm{vol}(b)} \tag{4.5}$$

This portfolio has access to the ambient shortcuts while staying within the
"relevant" region of the simplex.

**Regret hierarchy:**

$$0 = \text{Regret}_{\rm Kelly} \leq \frac{r\log T}{2T} = \text{Regret}_{\rm MUP}
\leq \frac{(r+k)\log T}{2T} = \text{Regret}_{\rm CHP}
\leq \frac{(d-1)\log T}{2T} = \text{Regret}_{\rm Cover} \tag{4.6}$$

where $k = \dim(\text{conv}(M^r)) - r$ is the number of ambient shortcut dimensions.

For high-curvature manifolds: $k$ is small and the shortcut benefit is large
→ CHP outperforms MUP after accounting for the ambient gain. For flat manifolds:
$k = 0$ and CHP = MUP.

## 5. The CFL Condition for Market Observation

### 5.1 The discrete speed of information

On a Voronoi tessellation with $N$ cells, the maximum information propagation
speed is:

$$c_M = \frac{\text{diam}_{\rm FR}(M)}{\text{diam}_{G} \cdot \Delta t} \tag{5.1}$$

where $\text{diam}_{G}$ is the graph diameter of the Delaunay graph and
$\Delta t$ is the observation interval.

The Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL) condition:

$$\Delta t \leq \frac{\Delta x_{\rm cell}}{c_M} \tag{5.2}$$

**You cannot update your portfolio faster than information crosses one
Voronoi cell.** This is the geometric minimum rebalancing interval.

### 5.2 The resolution-speed tradeoff

More Voronoi cells (finer mesh): better resolution but slower propagation
($\text{diam}_{G}$ increases).

Fewer cells (coarser mesh): faster propagation but worse resolution.

$$\text{Resolution} \times \text{Speed} \leq \text{const} \tag{5.3}$$

This is an UNCERTAINTY PRINCIPLE for market observation: you cannot
simultaneously have maximum spatial resolution AND maximum temporal
resolution on the manifold.

## 6. New Results

**Theorem O1** *(Observer's price is position-dependent)*.
The Fisher-Rao metric $g^{\rm FR}_{ij}(b) = \delta_{ij}/b_i$ at position
$b$ determines the observer's price sensitivity. Two observers at different
positions experience different markets.

**Theorem O2** *(The filtration hierarchy)*.
$\mathcal{F}^{\rm trade}_t \subsetneq \mathcal{F}^{A \cap B}_t \subsetneq
\mathcal{F}^A_t \cap \mathcal{F}^B_t$ — strict inclusions at every trade.

**Theorem O3** *(Every trader is an intermarket)*.
The market is a connected sum of trader manifolds, with trades as necks.
The market's spectral gap is the minimum over all component gaps and neck modes.

**Theorem O4** *(Ambient shortcut capacity ∝ Willmore energy)*.
The information capacity of the ambient channel is $C_{\rm shortcut} \propto \mathcal{W}$.
A perfectly efficient market has zero shortcut capacity.

**Theorem O5** *(Optimal inefficiency)*.
The information-optimal market has $\mathcal{W}^{\ast} > 0$ — strictly positive
Willmore energy. Perfect efficiency is suboptimal for information processing.

**Theorem O6** *(CFL condition for portfolios)*.
The minimum rebalancing interval is $\Delta t_{\rm min} = \Delta x_{\rm cell}/c_M$
— one Voronoi cell crossing time.
